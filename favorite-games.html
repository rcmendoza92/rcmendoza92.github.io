    Action games

    collision · ballistics · kung fu triage · chaos

    1. Resident Evil 4

      Capcom 2005

      A beat 'em up disguised as a shooting gallery disguised as Sweet Home by way of laser­disc Gymkata; a fork­lift tachanka dark ride meta­sta­sizing corner­ed animal panic into round­house kicking bliss.

    2. Ketsui: Kizuna Jigoku Tachi

      Cave 2003

      The ultimate in non­stop, over­whelming, balls to the wall aggression.

      Devilishly elegant option lock mech­anic, compulsively swash­buckling scoring system, mesmerizing, tentacular cotton candy bullet swarms: a suicide mission worth accept­ing again & again & again.

    3. DoDonPachi DaiOuJou

      Cave 2002

      A hypnotic, pulse-pounding rebuttal — thanks in large part to the flexi­bility afforded by the end­less­ly subtle hyper system — to the notion that auto­scroll­ng manic shoot­ers are simply a matter of de­viating as little as possible from a single obvi­ous, opti­mal route; breath­ing deep of the naphtha fumes from a pre­cision-engi­neered master­piece like DOJ will have you see­ing level design in a totally new light.

      Sous Lunopolis, l'abeilles!

    4. Alien Soldier

      Treasure 1995

      Psychotic & relent­less: a larynx-tearing Z80 scream of desp­erate tech­nical whiz­bang, nasty, cramped, deliri­ously imagin­ative boss fights, and the most night­marish­ly de­ranged open­ing text crawl in science fiction.

    5. God Hand

      Clover 2006

      A full-body DualShock work­out where the mash­ing feels axio­matic?

      Just that first level is so much fun to faff about in; some of the funniest mooks in video games.

    6. Demon's Souls

      From Software 2009

      3D GnG + OSR DnD (Berserk module): tip­toe­ing down a slimy cat­walk behind an allegory-painted pavise, remin­iscing on having a body, wonder­ing how a ghost can con­tract malaria.

      Of all the gimmick gaunt­lets re­tread in later Souls­bornes, why not the giant explo­ding pill­bug tunnels from 2-2? You guys don't like fun?

    7. Alien vs. Predator

      Capcom 1994

      It feels so good bully­ing these screen-filling hordes of massive, ugly, beautiful sprites: juggles, pogos, OTGs, air throws, pile drivers, wall kicks, back­dashes, over­heat cancels; more free­style than Final Fight but still a flinty, claustro­phobic tact­ical challenge.

    8. Bloodborne

      From Software 2015

      College.

    9. Final Fight

      Capcom 1989

      Pitch-perfect cast, brutal simplicity, red hot CPS clavi-crunch: this thing's a laser beam.

    10. Devil May Cry 3

      Capcom 2005

      Cutscenes are good?

    11. Hyper Demon

      Sorath 2022

      Not only the contest­ant but the game it­self, Janus-like, has eyes in the back of its head, circle strafing the arena shooter's wave-containing past while peering through the firmament into its eldritch caravan future.

    12. Strider

      Capcom 1989

      I can vividly imagine having glimpsed this total dreamboat in some laundromat as a kid:

      You were like this futuristic ninja who could slide, and climb walls, and walk up slopes and it looked totally natural — the graphics were unreal — there were like vines that would bend under your weight, and cutscenes with characters speaking all these different languages, and one swipe of your beam sword would cut bad guys in half... it had this kind of exotic soviet mosque thing going on... and there was this wrestler you'd have to fight at the bottom of the first stage if you missed a jump... God, what was it??

    Adventure games

    stealth · survival · scavenging · knapsack packing

    1. Metal Gear Solid 3: Snake Eater

      Konami 2004

      The last time I slith­ered through this sumptuous­ly pedant­ic spy serial phantasm­agoria, I was over­come with emotion at the mangrove swamp bardo sequence — or rather, just after, when Naked Snake finds him­self in Tikhogornyj (Тихогорный, tranq­uil mount­ain), a seclu­ded komorebi plunge pool pres­ided over by a Japanese fly­ing squirrel.

      Photo­synthesis... Guy Savage... Time Paradox...

      Will we ever see its like again?

    2. Pathologic 2

      Ice-Pick Lodge 2019

      If a role­playing game does­n't feature a de­tail­ed model for combat, or spelunk­ing, or camp­aign­ing, or diplo­macy; if the player does­n't share their char­acter's talents, or know­ledge, or hamartia; has no means to mechanically ex­press their ident­ity — then what's left? just cos­play?

      What if a role's dist­inct­ion — its proj­ect­ive provo­cation — isn't about pre­tend­ing to ex­otic apt­itudes, accoutre­ments & witty barbs but an act­ual role in an act­ual drama: a part to play — to learn; a mise en abyme of others' ex­pect­ations & preju­dices; a carillon of commit­ments, social ties, priv­ileges, herit­age.

      What if the con­comit­ant mini­games were­n't chival­ric vaude­ville but a matrix of pain­ful trade-offs, an im­poss­ibly murky triage puzzle: season­al loot­ing, sand­lot sleuth­ing, house call itiner­aries, herbal harv­ests, hospi­tal work, couch surf­ing, barter qua demo­graph­ic arbit­rage — oh, and basic self-pre­ser­vation.

      Far & away the best CRPG ever made; may­be the only one that act­ually works for me as such, be­cause it forces me to do some­thing none other has the chutzpah to: care.

    3. Silent Hill 2

      Team Silent 2001

      Jeremy Blaustein's best script: a dark night of the soul laying spind­ly, loving fingers on topics still too taboo for this pansy new form of cinema & brought to sham­bling, un­canny life by the most touch­ingly awk­ward, sizz­ling­ly close-miked VA (Monica Horgan's wist­ful sibil­ants, Donna Burke's disconcert­ing cadences, Guy Cihi's benthic, bathetic rumble) ever commit­ed to opti­cal disc.

      Some­how hours spent jigg­ling broken locks in grungy hall­ways & un­clogg­ing garbage chutes with six-packs of apple juice link to­gether into an un­broken string of black­est black pearls; the ulti­mate sad sack stay­cation.

    4. Ico

      Sony Computer Entertainment 2001 

      Holding hands, banquette walks
Dove-white sands, silly talk
All I see are windmills and the queen
Who wants her own daughter for lunch
But we're having watermelon punch
Yeah, Yorda is the only one for me

    5. Papers, Please

      Lucas Pope 2013

      Simple documentary cross-referencing undergirds electronic gaming's most peremptory narrative pickle to date; the claims this simulation makes about the relationship between competence & compassion are disturbing, but not quite in the way many of its competitors' are.

    6. killer7

      Grasshopper Manufacture 2005

      Meat hooks hung with gray, naked child­ren's corpses — I guess the cens­ors at Nintendo finally caught up on their sleep.

    7. Anchorhead

      Micheal Gentry 1998

      The sort of sturd­ily written, superb­ly paced paragon of classi­cal advent­ure gaming — poised almost exact­ly between narrat­ive legato & puzzle­fest staccato — I wasn’t sure act­ually existed, but I’m glad it does; forgiv­ing a few fusty implement­ation quirks, the exper­ience of wander­ing through this drizzly New England town & un­cover­ing its dismal secrets is deeply satis­fying & pimpled with dark delights — dog skulls, spider­bites, peep­holes, ghost trains — slow­ly sed­ucing the player into its morbid frame of mind; despite the notes of fish oil, necrosis & ozone, it’s surpris­ingly fresh.

    8. The Legend of Zelda: A Link to the Past

      Nintendo EAD 1992

      TENTEN in full King of Cartoons regalia, trump­et­ing from the battle­ments an S-SMP fan­are heralding video games' sensuous adolescence.

    9. Thief II: The Metal Age

      Looking Glass 2000

      The 90's noirPG's chatoyant apotheosis.

    10. Half-Life 2

      Valve 2004

      Worse AI, worse level design, less coherent, less pointed, less scrappy, more talky, more self-serious, can't gib the scientists scientist.

      I like to pretend that Half-Life 2 is this black sheep — that it didn't influence any­thing except maybe some spooky bed­room dev walk­ing sims — that its un­canny, crepuscular photo­real corridor slither­ing was a one-off experi­ment, quickly abandoned.

      No, it's not IDM Die Hard, but it is — like Sofia, Bulgaria or Selected Ambient Works Volume II — haunted.

      Also, Water Hazard is my favorite map.

    11. Final Fantasy VI

      Squaresoft 1994

      Chibi steam­punk Star Wars, but they're all puppets — and with better music.

    12. Mystery Channel

      thecatamites 2013

      The actual scariest horror game: framed as a late-night TV mara­thon of slasher flicks, Mystery Channel is the grimm­est, bleak­est, least whim­sical belch of nausea in thecatamites' ludo­graphy; and it's here, at 3:45 am, 5:30 am, 6:15 am (I hear birds), that his Bernhard­ian sad­core dark­ness is at its dark­est & most dir­ect.

      Sleepless, picking at... nightmare scab visions...

      For one thing, the hypna­gogic stabs at empathy are genuine­ly dist­urb­ing: in P.J.R.I.P., the narr­ator (docu­mentary film­maker?) attempts to inter­view one of the un­stopp­able kill­ers, to no avail — the inter­viewee is incomp­rehens­ible & list­less; in Night of the Killers 2, we follow the capirote'd murder cult as they trudge back through the empty streets to their indi­vidual abodes, coast­ing on dawn en­ergy, lean­ing on each other for support; in Mask of Death, the narr­ator conf­esses that giv­ing up on runn­ing from the murder­er chas­ing him through his recurr­ing night­mare was also a way of demon­strat­ing savvy and matur­ity. I'd seen those movies. I knew what was up. — but every time, it's foll­owed by the horr­ible reali­zation of having fore­closed on the possib­ility of escape.

    Puzzle & Strategy games

    sequencing · sorting · synergy · logistics

    1. Cinco Paus

      Michael Brough 2017

      Magnifies Rogue's wand ID mini­game until it combusts, re­leasing the phlogiston of a vingt-cinq Eleusis.

      And it's not just a trigger hazel-happy gambling den fronting an Erol Otus alchemy lab — there's layers to this merda; if learn­ing is fun, Cinco Paus is Pancake Tuesday.

    2. Imbroglio

      Michael Brough 2015

      Like Radiant Silvergun, squeezes an epic RPG leveling arc into a choking hazard-compact hour at the arcade.

      Wall shuffle = Deck shuffle hits like a scient­ific discovery, and sets the stage for a darkly roborant sow & reap rubato; a cons­truct­ed format com­pulsion to equal MtG: Arena, a one-man one-thumb touch­screen wonder, and the medium's most pro­found & pyro­lytic medi­tation on parity.

    3. Corrypt

      Michael Brough 2012

      The un­hinged culmin­ation of Michael Brough's Zelda-by-worm-light explor­ation kicked off with Game Title & steered into an­other dimension by its glitch-harness­ing follow-up Lost Levels.

      I must have beat­en this thing in a fugue state: twists an already-clever (if dry) Chip's Challenge adventure into a Rubik's tetra­hemi­hexa­hedron night­mare of your own making, with an under­world that feels like stumbling through a dank, un­swept debug storage closet.

    4. 868-HACK

      Michael Brough 2014

      I've long maintained that Forth implement­ations would benefit from a stun gun.

      I always get .SHOW because I like the Ozric Tentacles synths & be­cause Cryptogs give me heart attacks.

      Do you know how to reach the secret level?

    5. Spelunky HD

      Mossmouth 2012

      All roads lead to Spelunky.

    6. Brogue

      Brian Walker 2009

      UV-svelte pressure plate stepping with chess-y weapons, ASCII tremens, vola­tile gases & flamm­able grasses: the Braun radio of rogue­likes.

    7. Advance Wars

      Intelligent Systems 2001

      Nothing settles the nerves like a pint of combined arms Neopolitan.

    8. Stephen's Sausage Roll

      Increpare 2016

      Leave it to Dr PuzzleScript to deliver the most dia­bolical Sokoban suite in the history of logistics.

    9. Bean and Nothingness

      RBOR Games 2021

      Adorable aporia.

    10. Elebits

      Konami 2006

      Turning a child's bedroom upside-down in quest of batteries: the rental game personified.

    Racing & Climbing games

    gravity · momentum · traction · acrobatics

    1. Super Metroid

      Nintendo R&D1 1994

      First it's marooned Pellucidar plumb­ing, then it's a collider-compress­ing mock­ball race to mother­hood; Nintendo's moody subterranean summit.

      Ghost ships — flooded caves — re­friger­ated mazes — a Red Tower as X-ray shadow­graphic as de Chirico's — those CCTVs in the war room with the Metal Pirates...

      Pure class.

    2. Shadow of the Colossus

      Sony Computer Entertainment 2005

      Agro the horse is a treasure of anim­ation, and the colossi them­elves are the cool­est monsters in... any­thing: #1's balcony, #4's braids, #12's molars; the most thrilling slide­show I've ever sat through.

    3. Rain World

      Videocult 2017

      Like some Flash refuse­nik's Pac-Metroid hekhalot: a swelt­er­ing post­human wild­style cathedral; a 16 hun­dred screen sprawl of rubb­ish, plumb­ing, rebar & radio towers; and a jarr­ing­ly eidetic xeno­zoo zoe­trope, I suspect in part be­cause it in­sists on animalia's character­istic sus­pension of awk­ward­ness & grace.

      Surpris­ingly tech­nical, admir­ably in­diff­erent, anec­dotally fecund — regurgi­tated flash­bangs, deimatic vulture mask E-ZPasses, slo-mo mush­room lant­ern heists, snack­ing on bats & pop­corn while watch­ing a tangle of irascible liz­ards tumble off a truss — an un­forgettable summer in the city.

    4. Super Mario World

      Nintendo EAD 1990

      Raigan Burns on World's inertia:

      It's fundamental: for Mario, it makes more of the magic occur in your brain and less just in the computer and on the screen, because it's all about the anticipation, and it's kind of like a proprioception, which is like you know where your limbs are even if you can't see them; and it's like a digital prosthetic...

    5. Tony Hawk's Pro Skater 2

      Neversoft 2000

      A mere olympiad into its ex­ist­ence, the 3D plat­former argu­ably perf­ected; like a ska punk harus­pex, you'll read the lines.

    6. Super Mario 64

      Nintendo EAD 1996

      Thumb­stick puppetry in N-World dio­ramas un­cork­ing still-effervescent gym­nastic jubil­ation.

    7. Katamari Damacy

      Namco 2004

      The only fantasy I assume to be truly uni­versal; syzygetic pop art catharsis.

    8. Sylvie Lime

      sylvie 2022

      Some days she's a blazing doujin soft maverick, some days she's a lime.

      Exchanging tips with other players at the last(?) in-person Babycastles event was peak neo-arcade.

    9. Flywrench

      Messhof 2015

      My maso­core plat­former of choice: a crazily kinetic, whining-sharp wire course juke set; Flappy Bird spotted candy flipp­ing in Chicago.

    10. Lonely Mountains: Downhill

      Megagon Industries 2019

      Tank controls + tangency: a match made in heaven.

    11. Super Mario World 2: Yoshi's Island

      Nintendo EAD 1995

      The physics! The trick shots!

      Such a stylish & entertaining speed game — a showcase for gobsmacking dexterity on the part of programmer and player.

    12. Grow Home

      Ubisoft Reflections 2015

      Petals peeling from your marguerite parachute as you plummet all the way back 2 the bean-base: what video game UI should be.

    Competitive games

    esports · spectating · teamwork · yomi

    1. Super Smash Bros. Melee

      HAL Laboratory 2001

      The most inexplic­able, inexhaust­ible, sauciest Lylatian salsa ever to crash-land into unsus­pect­ing mill­ennials' digi­tal memory.

      Main: Samus
      Secondaries: Peach Daisy, Sheik, Young Link

    2. SpyParty

      Chris Hecker 2018

      To quote yours truly:

      Rather than pro­voking bore­dom, its taste­fully bland, thorough­ly decent urban­ity is re­fresh­ing, even brave; more re­fresh­ing still is the ex­perience of act­ually play­ing it, of meet­ing its re­spect­ful demand for your com­plete attent­ion, massag­ing out the de­fault pro­gram of ab­surd fidget­ing that other games have rout­ed through your peev­ish little brain.

    3. Quake Live

      id Software 2010

      10-minute duel is such an under­appreciated waltz: the un­holy trinity of rockets (area denial), shaft (DPS tickle) & rail (hit­scan lump sum); con­trol (or cycling the health item spawns); play­ing squirrely pot­shot keep­away when out of control; desper­ate rocket jump into mega steal gambits — Rapha-level Q3/Live's cat & mouse skirm­ish­ing may just house the hypest game­play known to homo ludens.

    4. Guilty Gear XX Accent Core +R

      Arc System Works 2012

      Shrill, baroque, dis­honest double air­dash anime bull­shit.

      Main: Baiken
      Secondaries: Venom, Order-Sol

    5. Intruder

      Superboss games 2019

      Simul­taneously a rag­doll + spatial audio comedy gold­mine & a canny dist­illation of the stealth-oriented immers­ive sim into an elite platoon of adorable mech­anics: walkie-talkie, peri­scope, zip­line, lock­pick, banana peel, card­board decoy and — nat­urally — fully analog door hinges.

    6. Killer Queen

      BumbleBear 2013

      Tecate in cozy, lever grip on wine­glass, I'm on snail! — what MOBAs always should have been.

      I asked Nik Mikros if he'd add turbo & he said no.

    7. Nock

      Normal 2022

      Dragging yourself along the low-friction pitch & dribbling with missiles already feels incredible, but turning your mid-flight whiff into a floating orientable cube is exquisite.

      Rocket League never quite clicked for me but this one keeps me stomping on 10 year olds for hours.

    8. StarCraft: Brood War

      Blizzard 1998

      Tastosis have some­thing that can never be repli­cated; though I have no intent­ion of baby­sitting prod­uction cycles & clump­ing Mutalisks myself, their enthus­iasm is infectious.

    9. Botolo

      Auren Snyder 2016

      Minimalist fight­ing's most egregious­ly slept-on under­dog: nothing but mind­games, but it's also act­ually fun.

      The roster of classes is gidd­ily in­vent­ive & deli­ciously trick­sy — this thing needs a slight­ly less puritan sequel (Botwolo?), or at the very least some side stage notor­iety.

    10. No Brakes Valet

      Justin Smith 2014

      The gut-busting nexus of Jet de Go!, regular Go & WarioWare.

    Tabletop games

    board · card · party · abstract

    1. Netrunner

      Richard Garfield, Lukas Litzinger

      Fiber bundle Dungeon Keeper meets Texas Hold 'Em money­ball; a great strat­egy RPG and a great competitive bluff­ing game; a crazy-cool '90s concept and a cogent 2010s consummation.

    2. Tigris & Euphrates

      Reiner Knizia

      Now this is a board game: a set-sowing cell­ular auction squeezing Francis Tresham & Colonel Blotto into 4-way teutonic Go; or as BGG user Luke @toblerdrone puts it:

      a combin­ation of crystalline balance and what I would polite­ly call emergent psyche­delic madness.

    3. Race for the Galaxy

      Thomas Lehmann

      The quick­fire Euro perfected.

    4. Codenames

      Vlaada Chvátil

      A truly co­öperative meaning-mine­field rendez­vous problem; authent­ically captures some­thing of the brain­feel of writing poetry.

      Mike Zapawa

      Ingenious LoS-based placement & capture protocol; maybe the least compromised Chess–Go compromise yet, with a corrosive attrition dynamic not quite found in either.

    6. Go

      anonymous

    7. Le Havre

      Uwe Rosenberg

    8. Pax Porfiriana

      Matt & Phil Eklund

      Teddy Roosevelt bishōjo Fluxx, with flavor text that's actually worth reading.
