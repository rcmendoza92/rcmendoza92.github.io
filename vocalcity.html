    Vocalcity at 25

    Sometime in 2022 I became frank­ly obsess­ed with Vocalcity, Finn­ish prod­ucer Luomo's (aka Vladislav Delay aka Sasu Ripatti) 2000 debut.

    I've histor­ic­ally been pretty partial to what's called micro­house — and after all, that makes sense; if it's the meet­ing place for mini­mal tech­no, dub, glitch & deep house then yeah, let me in — and Vocalcity is wide­ly consid­erend a mile­stone in the sub­genre's develop­ment.

    Back when I start­ed getting into elec­tronic music (we're talking prob­ably 2010), I must have come across Vocalcity on some best of the 2000s lists, scrubbed through 4 or 5 of its 6 10-minute-plus tracks & sort of wond­ered what the fuss was about.

    Skimming the surface, it prob­ably sound­ed mono­tonous (oh no!), reticent, schol­astic & unutter­ably European — like a bio­fuel-blooded battery of stain­less steel pistons pump­ing away at nothing, domin­ated by the sort of jejune synth vamps which wriggle their way through the begin­ning of its first track, Market.

    Well, I've given the whole thing many attent­ive listens now and I think it may just be the best dance long-player ever made — at least for my purp­oses.

    It braids to­gether all these wonder­ful strands of electro­nic music — dub techno's roil­ing depth, glitch's CD-spectral near-plane surgery, house's huffy disco dream­scapes — in such a serene, almost bland way, per­fectly poised be­tween Mid­west grit & Nordic glamour — it doesn't really feel con­trived or even particul­arly provoc­ative; but the gen­eral form is these long, tectonic­ally shift­ing grooves — at first feel­ing like exer­cises — warmups — itchy, a little slinky, insist­ent... and then, at their own org­anic, self-assured pace — and with unim­peachable timing — they mature into these deep, immer­sive, deeply sexy & gener­ous medit­ations.

    In the end Vocalcity doesn't really sound micro at all — it sounds mass­ive, envelop­ing, circad­ian; and what seemed like end­less fore­play, if you stick it out, winds up punch­ing a ticket to honest-to-god cathar­sis.

    One parti­cularly aud­acious trick that really gets my goose­bumps bump­ing is how it'll fore­shadow some vocal pay­load — a full track in ad­vance some­times — sending it flutter­ing through the presque vu peri­phery like an ori­gami dove; or when the vocals do enter in earn­est, the keys might drag a step be­hind, the kick drum follow­ing later still.

    These teases & tugs are sort of EDM's bread & butter, but in Ripatti's hands this stuff isn't cheap, it's ex­quisite — virtu­osic even — the man indeed.

    Market

    We kick things off with that sticky stab + bend synth riff, Morse code bass tapping on your fore­head.

    Sharp in­takes of breath; a sunny warble like Snell's window; faint, spiral­ing whines; a pele­ton of bike gear tune-ups.

    It's not until 4:48 that a vocal at the verb­al stage even enters, after which the rhythm takes on an ex­plicit 2-step shuffle.

    At 8:16 the tele­phonic e­piano drops out for a moment and every­thing relaxes into an aqua­rian languor.

    At 9:54, the synth from earlier re­turns, but pitched higher, per­forming a little seesaw parody of its orig­inal ostinato, before the track ultim­ately dissi­pates, glow­ing with sero­tonin, into its achey denoue­ment.

    Class

    Class might be the best ex­ample of how Vocalcity re­wards your patience, how it evolves what could be workman­like techno clichés into chrism­al, comely, volupt­iously filli­greed dance­floor sex-vespers.

    After the first four minutes' jazzy, in­souciant e­piano, paper­y snare-claps, froggy synth cate­chisms & subtly stacked vocal re­frain (Nothing to do others can't do), the track drops into a spartan, hard-stepping funk drill.

    But then at 4:51, the drums briefly spin back­wards, a female coo soft­ly pre­sides, and we shift almost sub­limin­ally into an­other chord: a balmy, purple C-sharp minor 9.

    It's almost shock­ing to me how deft & power­ful that moment is; it's really, really funky.

    Synkro

    Prob­ably the most repet­itive track on the album — 14 minutes of tauto­logical come-ons & pump­ing dia­phragm-like bass — but oh my lord is it just slow-cooked deep house per­fection.

    Against a vortex of dubby creaks & scrapes bubbling with reedy sax quacks, Ripatti mass­ages from a Rhodes these glib, blues­y mordents.

    On Pangea time, the groove un­furls: the cicada chirp of a hallucin­ogenic­ally tact­ile 303 — backing female vocals echo­ing past in half-silvered frag­ments — at 10:14, an industrial-scale steam-sigh border­ing on in­decency.

    Stoner­ific dub canyons carved out with the phero­mone-laced sweat of the deep­est, most calli­pygian house.

    The Right Wing

    Track 4 I for a while just thought of as a bridge be­tween the mesmeric Synkro & the titanic Tessio; but late­ly I've come to re­gard it as a connoisseur's case for the dist­inct pleasures of micro­house: a burb­ling, phlegm­atic & yet strange­ly genial jab of histamine.

    It certain­ly nails the details: that guiro-beetle rattle, crump­led trump­et, noirish tine piano, vinyl wib-wub zipper.

    But it’s that long, diffuse, dream-logic tail wind­ing into the next track — a woman raving (she don't even notice me when I'm inside her soul), an­other sniff­ling, airy pads, a phone ring­ing — and those eerie, grainy pre­views of the chorus to come — that really gives it its pur­pose.

    Tessio

    Here we are then: Tessio.

    What a climax; truly one of the all-time great dance­floor anthems.

    It builds so beauti­fully — acid bass swell­ing, woozy & elast­ic — stray voices flicker­ing like candle flames at a séance — an open hi-hat's quick­ly cut-off sh! — and when it gets there it delivers.

    Male & female final­ly con­join­ed; 6:28's disco­delic tutti sting; 9 minutes in, the super­heroic barn­storm­ing of a Staples-style diva drenched in cathedral re­verb.

    An almost brazen venture into senti­mental electro­pop ballad­ry, but re­tain­ing all the surround­ing sprezz­atura (the kick­drum doesn’t hit until 2 minutes in, the full four-to-the-floor 20 seconds after that), it’s with­out a doubt Vocalcity’s (if not millennial EDM's) crown jewel & a triumph of soul in electro­nic music — the real thing.

    She-Center

    Luomo closes the album out with a neon-streaked fly­ing taxi ride: feline e­piano, zips & zaps, wine-dark pads, the reggae-est bass organ yet.

    A come-hither harem of R&B vocal­ics; a cacoph­onous clatter of Scientist-style SFX; finally, the ivory dry­ness of a muted nylon guitar & last squelch­ing stabs of that ever-present 303.

    It’s awesome stuff; and though I haven’t been spinn­ing it for 25 years straight, I have to imag­ine it’s an un­usually sturdy & re­liable dance record.

    Swollen, tact­ile, aspir­ated, sultry — to me Vocalcity may be the album that best delivers on the prom­ise of what a long-play­ing dance record can be: simult­aneous­ly fractal cent­ri­fuge & time-lapse fjord — this trance-inducing yet infin­itely ex­plorable psych­edelic bio­mechan­ical insect­oid sex arc­ology... thing.

    Don’t skim; get in there — it’s deep.

    July 2025

    Robin Mendoza
