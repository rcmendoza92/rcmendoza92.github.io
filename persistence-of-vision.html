    • Persistence of Vision

    Iridian fog pours off green wheat shadowed by palm fronds; near­by, an ancient via­duct spans the baby blue ice cre­vasse, lead­ing event­ually to a cliff-hung sand­stone kiosk.

    Caped figures scuttle between wind­mills on clay battle­ments, rain­ing down ray­gun fire on a terrace where rit­ual braz­iers burn candy apple red; below, a tang­erine valley cradles col­onnaded buttes, rock-cut doors, store-caves, a flower harvester...

    Wherever this world is, I’m not there right now; in fact, I never really did get to visit.

    I’m day­dreaming from a travel­ogue, collating stray impress­ions caught from the nacre­ous air; I’m watch­ing Love Gameplay HD, My hastely made Love techtree intro, Love Alpha – Bali Chill Groove (Time Lapse).

    I’m stuck on an inter­minable start screen — a fly­over of some myst­erious planet strung with pennant banners & chase lights — look­ing for a server voucher on a Way­back Mach­ine snap­shot of gaminglove.net circa 2012...

    What is Love? Put simply, it’s a now-dormant massively moder­ately multi­player FPS-RTS whats­it written from scratch, in C, by a lone Swedish pro­grammer named Eskil Steenberg (dba Quel Solaar).

    A hugely ambitious project, Love began develop­ment some­time around 2006, with its first pub­lic release, an alpha build which could be down­loaded from Quel Solaar’s website for €3, appear­ing in October 2009 – more than half a year before Notch an­nounced the re­lease of Minecraft’s alpha in June 2010.

    Why Minecraft? Well, they’re both indie first-person sand­box build­ing games with proced­urally gen­erated worlds, deform­able terrain, multi­player servers, styl­ized graphics, dyn­amic weather, day-night cycles, craft­ing systems… both were ins­pired by Zach & Tarn Adams’ Dwarf Fortress – and, Notch and Steenberg are fellow Swedes; Notch even asked Steenberg – an early member of OpenGL’s Architectural Review board – for assist­ance in implem­enting some OpenGL exten­sions on the notor­iously Java-based Minecraft.

    Take one look at Love & Steenberg’s wizardry as a graphics researcher shows:

    • Four-colored fog (near, far, toward sun, away from sun)
    • Anamorphic lens flare
    • Indirect illumination
    • Cloth simulation
    • Inverse kinematics
    • Splatting
    • Bokeh-like particle emitters
    • A faintly strobing noise filter.

    Though the geo­metry is crude, though those sur­faces are descr­ibed with flat colors ins­tead of dense, photo­graphic text­ures, and though the over­all image is soft and smudgy (and prone to give some players head­aches), Love’s visual gambit pays off with a hallucin­atory impress­ionism that's dynamic, begui­ling, prim­eval & aeol­ian yet vaguely Star Wars, and spang­led with ex­amples of un­usual tech­nical ingen­uity – pro­grammer art in the most ad­miring sense of the term.

    A canopy flaps in the same wind that prop­agates a brush fire.

    Blaster beams cast light be­neath them­selves as they cleave the earth from already-erod­ing can­yons.

    Weather ball­oons sail through curli­cue clouds, over a curved hori­zon.

    It’s not merely the opti­cal god that Steenberg’s tech­nical cunning serves; the whole idea be­hind Love, in a sense, is to bring its constit­uent parts into fluent conv­ersation with each other.

    Whatever features Steenberg decided to imple­ment are inten­ded to multi­ply the means by which Love’s players can inter­act with each other & with the world: thus wind­mills can be built to gen­erate power, to spread fires & to push ball­oons; radio freq­uencies can be set up to listen for rem­ote acti­vation codes as well as to secure priv­ate chat channels for ex­chang­ing crit­ical coörd­inates; those same coörd­inates can be access­ed from Doom 3-style mouse­look-driven video term­inals; the hori­zon curves be­cause the plan­et is act­ually spher­ical (the in-game map is even a globe holo­gram) – go far enough in one dir­ection & you’ll end up where you start­ed; like­wise, what’s day on one side will be night on the other.

    This mono­modal, syst­emic approach, the sort that won con­verts more recent­ly with titles like Breath of the Wild – in con­cert with the strange­ly lo-fi yet high tech graphics – prov­okes a feel­ing of fresh­ness & sincer­ity that’s linger­ed in my mind ever since first encount­ering the game in maybe 2011 or 2012, though I don't re­call if that was on Rock Paper Shotgun or the pion­eering VG photo­graphy blog Dead End Thrills.

    Of course the irony’s not lost on me that a game whose ex­press mission was to counter the pre­vail­ing trend of video game as amuse­ment park ride – to offer no cine­matic exper­iences save those that em­erge organic­ally from the con­fluence of syst­ems & player agency – is for me this evan­escent non-place re­turned to only in 480p You­Tube clips and decade-old web­zine screen­shots.

    In some ways that fugi­tive leg­acy is apt, though, since Love never really mat­ured past its embry­onic prom­ise into an act­ual game qua game.

    In the days be­fore Early Access, Love was pro­posed as a contin­uously exp­anded & re­fined experi­mental mini-MMO (about 200 users per server) whose player base – their disco­veries, procliv­ities, emer­gent social dyn­amics – would guide & shape the course of the proj­ect’s develop­ment.

    Even now it’s hard for me to give – or guess – the abs­tract for Love’s meta­game.

    Ok, a player gets a voucher, joins a server, and is dropped into an inst­ance of this dreamy alien world – about 1.5 km in circum­ference; suppos­edly they look around for a mono­lith, a special token which is driven into the ground to found a settle­ment; they meet up with other play­ers on the server & whether alone or as a party exp­lore the envi­rons & raid NPC fort­resses to bring mater­ials (min­eral, clay, flower, water, gas & anti­matter) & techno­logies (weapons & tokens) back to their settle­ment to im­prove it; the text crawl on the start screen ment­ions some MacGuffin – The Arti­fact – which I think may­be one of the five AI tribes is hoard­ing? bring it back to your settle­ment?

    Even if play­ers follow this gen­eral loop, what’s the end­game? Steenberg was ada­mant when design­ing Love that there be no hard-coded player pro­gression: no level­ing, no rank­ing – heck, no priv­ate prop­erty – the idea being to have a comm­unal invent­ory that play­ers share, situ­ated immed­iately with­in & on top of the actual 3D environ­ment.

    Fair enough but, especi­ally with­out the usual RPG carrots, it seems the engine for player motiv­ation never really got locked in, whether the intent­ion was for per­petual sand­castle re­build­ing or a finite space opera grail quest – and neither were there ever enough dedic­ated play­ers to write their own epic poli­tical cons­piracy à la EVE Online.

    In a 2011 preview for Rock Paper Shotgun, Quintin Smith exp­ressed his con­cern that play­ers might quick­ly run out of things to do & that, while Steenberg was push­ing Love as a co­öper­ative game, Smith be­lieved that the frame­work might better support comp­etitive multi­player:

    You could have mult­iple forts of pioneers duking it out, cutting one an­other’s power, or infil­trating the oppos­ition’s base to re­direct their new art­illery piece at their allies.

    For all of Love’s pre­science, it seems it came just shy of pred­icting what Morgan Park in PC Gamer called the raid game: the shoot & loot PvPvE form­at exemp­lified by post-PUBG eff­orts like Hunt: Showdown & Escape from Tarkov.

    This new breed of shooter bridges the gaps be­tween round-based player elimin­ation, resource-ratio­ning surv­ival & MMO-like persist­ence & quests; per­haps some sim­ilar config­uration could have served Love better than the hand­waved hippie dippy mech­anism design under which it ulti­mately fizz­led out.

    Still, I cert­ainly have more conf­idence in the fract­al design appr­oach Steenberg en­dorses than in the mini­game buffet of some­thing like Spore.

    Steenberg:

    Why not make a real world with a sort of cause and effect, like a strat­egy game, so the game is sort of like a strat­egy game where you play first person as one of the units. Then on top of that you have a larger obj­ective which is sort of like your civil­ization game. So you imag­ine a civil­ization v. a simul­ation game, and each battle is an RTS game, and then with­in that each unit is an FPS game.

    Though Love may have strugg­led to find its core drama­tic hook, there can be no doubt that it stuck to its guns blasters.

    It is soft­ware as art, built from first princi­ples; look around at the other proj­ects on Quel Solaar’s website & you’ll see much the same – but, per­haps even more impress­ively, these other offer­ings aren’t games but tools: tools as art; as toys, provoc­ations, creat­ive wedges for dis­lodging accept­ed wis­dom.

    Loq Airou is a 3D model­ing pro­gram control­led sole­ly with the mouse; rather than poly­gon meshes or NURBS, it uses creased sub­division sur­faces to de­scribe obj­ects, and, with the help of snap-to-vertex & a radial menu brought up with the middle mouse button, funct­ions as a sort of sketch­pad for geo­metry – the very same geo­metry Steenberg ported straight into Love, with no further detail­ing in a sec­ond appli­cation like, say, Blender.

    If you watch him demo the pro­gram you’ll see a dist­inct lack of tool­bars, drop­down menus, or really any window­ing at all; in­stead you’ll see a wire­frame cage that looks to be made of lasers, a smooth blue shaded shell, face select­ion widg­ets in the form of trans­lucent patches of CMY hex grid & edit actions con­firmed with indigo-fringed ana­morphic flares.

    Ministry of Flat: an auto­mated UV unwrapper.

    Seduce: an edit­or for 3D GUI widg­ets.

    Confuse: a pro­ced­ural char­acter anim­ation lib­rary/viewer.

    Nil Salentin: a mini­mal non­linear video editor.

    Stellar: a light con­troller.

    And on top of all this is Quel Solaar’s cus­tom net­work proto­col Verse; pro­grams connec­ted to a Verse server share mut­able data be­tween each other in real-time, incl­uding 3D obj­ects, so that any edits made in one pro­gram will prop­agate inst­antly to an­other – pre­figuring today’s trend, still in its adol­escence, to­ward virtu­alized geo­metry pipe­lines.

    Though I sus­pect they’re more or less un­usable to any­one but their inv­entor, each of these pro­grams – window­less, per­spect­ival, anim­ated (Most people are horri­fied by the idea of an anim­ated UI) – is a chall­enge to the reign­ing UI ortho­doxy set by Autodesk & Adobe.

    Designed to make the most common tasks im­mediate & joy­ful & to mini­mize distract­ions, the brazen, almost dream­like inter­faces in Quel Solaar’s cata­log are... aspir­ational.

    They’re cool – I mean, dorky, but cool – just so genuine­ly sci-fi: movie fanta­sies of soft­ware you can act­ually run on your crappy lap­top.

    I think it's that cock­tail of demo­scene brav­ura, Art Deco grand­eur & aff­ably passion­ate, slight­ly janky pie-in-the-sky obstin­ance that keeps me coming back to the what-ifs of Love & its creator.

    Much like he pers­isted toil­ing away in a small dark apart­ment in Sweden, deliver­ing up­dates like Avail & Dark Matter Spreads Over Fair Land to never more than a few hun­dred play­ers, noble fail­ures like Love per­sist in the fossil rec­ord, as it were, open­ing for the curious onto alter­native hist­ories.

    With its presc­ience & its ingen­uity, Love troub­les the recei­ved hist­ory of comp­uter gaming & its short­list of innovat­ors; and ten years if not more since first catch­ing sight of it on the blogs, it still mints an eer­ily serene and sui gen­eris mood, haunt­ing the heav­ens with zod­iacal dust from a gal­axy far, far away.
