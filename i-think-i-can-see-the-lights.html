    • I think I can see the lights

    Cass McCombs' County Line

    County Line, the single from Cass McCombs’ 2011 album Wit's End, was accomp­anied by two ent­irely differ­ent music videos.

    The first time I list­ened to this song, in 2011, it was watching the first video re­leased, direct­ed by Aaron Brown; some­time later, if mem­ory serves, this vid­eo was taken down from Domino's You­Tube chan­nel & re­placed with another before being reup­loaded on McCombs' own channel years later; but if you watch that orig­inal video, you might not be sur­prised to hear that it perman­ently color­ed my impress­ion of a piece of music which has pro­ved, for me, a cons­tant com­panion.

    Grisly & intimate but ultim­ately undis­closing, this muzzy, scuzzy DV montage of need­les, nood­les, bar burl­esques & box fans inst­antly set to boil­ing the ineff­able fore­boding simmer­ing under­neath the aw-shucks dirge it furn­ishes; and like the song it­self, it’s an enigma — one which, like a William Eggleston photo (or like his own night vision video­tape Stranded in Canton) threat­ens banal­ity as much as ab­jection — but I care, maybe more than I should — I feel spoken to, made privvy to some­thing — and I want to know.

    I can smell the columbine
    ... Colorado?

    In the language of flowers, the columbine is the emb­lem of de­ceived lovers, ingrat­itude, faith­less­ness.

    McCombs addre­sses his maldit, in a sur­pris­ing gesture of animism, to a county — not even a town.

    An alt-country affect­ation?

    An inver­sion of Chuck Berry’s paeans to driving?

    Or an admiss­ion of the sort of desult­ory sub­urban anonym­ity snaked through in Twin Peaks: The Return?

    You never even tried to love me he accu­ses in a sting­ing fals­etto, like a cotton ball soaked with alcohol; what­ever the source of his dis­appoint­ment, I feel it as my own: friends-of-friends & over­head lights & scuffed floors & Lustron houses among which you hoped you could sow love & comm­unity, some­how some­where let down on an un­shored drift to­ward per­fidy & obliv­ion.

    But follow­ing that bitter burp of chag­rin is a flare of warmth in the back of the throat — a cara­mel after­taste: pity.

    Though the emotion is diffi­cult, the color is fami­liar: brushed drums, that bleary Rhodes, an organ's prickly whine; the sound of driving back­ward into the past, like open­ing the door to your old apart­ment in a dream: more ambiv­alence than mere nost­algia.

    Better than any retro­grade parap­hrase I know of, County Line stows a dag­ger in the boot of shag­gy FM roots rock, arm­ing these aff­able cues with a poign­ant & cred­ible nau­sea.

    Partly this is down to the sand­washed subtle­ty of the accompan­iment: the penta­gram of left-hand e­piano desc­ending from the first invo­cation of the title; the lean into the organ which re­freshes the chorus half­way through; the bash­ful alter­nate pick­ing on the slight­ly out of tune guitar & then its spark­ing to life in the coda; the cresc­endo of tom hits under­lining columbine.

    Like Bill Eyden’s drudgy percus­sion on A Whiter Shade of Pale, the drums on County Line pave the narc­otic groove with a gris­tly artic­ulation — hesit­ating, almost amat­eurish at first blush, but as the song un­furls, these egg­shell taps & rum­pled fills re­veal, like an­other sec­ret, an exquis­itely sens­itive & dyn­amic Dixie soul feel.

    The march-like duple pulse seems to simult­aneously canter for­ward & stra­ggle behind, per­fect for conj­uring that twin sens­ation of prop­ulsion & iner­tia — of being pulled inex­orably back­ward — that is so key to the song’s planet-struck spell.

    This omi­nous note is sound­ed as soon as you hit play: dim­ming the lights with a bale­ful sub­mediant B minor (on my way), a twinge of mis­placed nost­algia moves us to the dom­inant A (old county); then we temp­orarily take refuge in a bitt­erly ironic tonic D (hoping nothing’s); wince with apprehen­sion at a sec­ondary dom­inant F-sharp major (changed); choke back the bile of a rue­ful super­tonic E minor (pain); and raise our gaze once again to the hori­zon’s in­finite A (never ending).

    A stunning cons­piracy of words & music — and weird­ly unique in Cass McCombs’ catalog; he has other good songs — Brighter!, Morning Shadows, I Went To The Hospital, It’s Getting Colder — but for all his occ­ult allus­ions & cape-twirls, there’s no­thing else this hyp­notic, this amaur­otic, this lived-in, with this vicar­ious power — no­thing that I hang from my rear­view mirror like my own per­sonal amu­let the way I do County Line.
